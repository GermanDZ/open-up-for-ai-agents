# Stakeholder Brief — ShareShed

*A minimal stakeholder input for a brand-new project entering Inception. This is
the raw material the Vision document should be derived from.*

## Project name

**ShareShed** — a neighborhood tool-lending web app.

## The situation (as told by stakeholders)

Most households own power tools and garden equipment they use a few times a year —
a drill, a pressure washer, a ladder, a hedge trimmer — that sit idle the rest of
the time. Meanwhile a neighbor two doors down buys the same thing for a single
weekend job. A local residents' association wants a simple way for verified
neighbors to **lend and borrow** physical tools within a small geographic
community, so people spend less, waste less, and meet their neighbors.

They tried a group chat and a shared spreadsheet. Both failed: no one could tell
what was available *right now*, items went out and never came back, and there was
no trust signal for a stranger three streets over.

## Who cares (stakeholders)

- **Borrowers** — residents who want to find and reserve a tool for a short period
  without buying it.
- **Lenders** — residents willing to lend their idle tools, but only if they can
  set availability, see who is borrowing, and get the item back on time.
- **Community administrator** — the residents' association volunteer who approves
  members, resolves disputes, and wants the scheme to actually get used.

## What they want (desired outcomes)

- See a **catalogue** of tools available nearby, with each item's condition and
  availability.
- **Reserve** an item for a date range and get a confirmation.
- **Return reminders** so borrowed items come back on time.
- A basic **trust signal** (verified membership + simple ratings) so lending to a
  near-stranger feels safe.
- The administrator can **approve members** and see what is currently on loan.

## Constraints & context

- Small scale to start: one neighborhood, tens to low-hundreds of members.
- Physical items only (no digital goods, no payments in the first version).
- Must work on a phone browser; most members are not tech enthusiasts.
- Success looks like: items actually circulate (utilization up), and borrowed
  items come back on time (low overdue rate), within the first season of use.

## Explicitly out of scope (for now)

- Payments, deposits, or insurance.
- Delivery/logistics — borrowers and lenders arrange handover themselves.
- Anything beyond a single neighborhood.
